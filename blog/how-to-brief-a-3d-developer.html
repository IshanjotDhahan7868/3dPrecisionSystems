How to Brief a 3D Developer: What You Need Before You Start

Most projects stall not because of budget or timeline — but because the client brief was vague. Here's the exact information a developer needs to get started fast.

ID
Ishanjot Dhahan
May 2, 2024 · 4 min read · Precision 3D Technologies

In every project we've taken on, the gap between a fast build and a slow build comes down to one thing: the quality of the brief.

We've started projects with a 10-page document of product specs, pricing logic, and option rules — and launched in 10 days. We've also started projects where three weeks in, we were still waiting on basic product dimensions. The project work is the same. The brief makes all the difference.

The Most Common Stall

The most common project stall we see has nothing to do with technical complexity or budget. It's the client not knowing what options their product actually has, or having that information locked in someone's head rather than in a document.

"We have a lot of options" is not a brief. "Here are 47 SKUs with dimensions, materials, and the pricing formula for each" is a brief. The difference between those two inputs is weeks of back-and-forth — or a clean first sprint.

1. Your Product Information

Before we can build anything, we need to understand what we're building. This means:

  • Product name and category
  • Key dimensions (what can the customer adjust, and what's the range?)
  • Photos from multiple angles — or ideally, existing 3D CAD files if you have them
  • A description of what the product looks like in each of its configurations
  • Any parts that are standard (always included) vs. optional

2. Option Logic

This is the most important and most often incomplete section of any brief. We need to know:

  • What options does the customer choose from? (Size, material, color, add-ons?)
  • Do any options depend on other options? (Example: louvered roof only available in aluminum)
  • Are there options that can't be combined? (Example: attached pergola requires minimum 10ft width)
  • What are the visual differences between options? (Different color = color change only? Different material = different 3D model?)

Saved on average when a client comes with a complete option matrix and pricing logic document vs. defining these during the build.

3. Pricing Structure

Real-time pricing is the most valuable feature in a configurator — and requires the most precise input. We need to know:

  • The base price for the minimum configuration
  • How each option affects the price (flat add-on? percentage? formula based on dimensions?)
  • Whether pricing should be shown as an estimate or firm quote
  • Any minimum order values or pricing rules

4. Integration Requirements

What happens after someone submits their configuration? We need to know:

  • Where should lead data go? (CRM, email, Slack notification?)
  • Should the submission generate a PDF quote?
  • Is there a booking tool (Calendly, etc.) to connect?
  • What analytics platform is in use?

5. Business Goals

Understanding why you're building the configurator shapes how we design it. The goals for a configurator meant to eliminate sales calls are different from one meant to increase average order value. Tell us what success looks like — we'll design toward it.

The Pre-Brief Checklist

Before your first call with us, try to have these ready:

  • Product photos (minimum 8, multiple angles)
  • Complete list of options with valid combinations
  • Pricing formula or spreadsheet
  • CRM or email platform for lead routing
  • One clear business metric you want to improve

If you have all of this ready, we can typically scope and start your project within the first week of engagement.
