We Built Our First Configurator. Here's Everything We Learned.

The first build is always the hardest. A candid look at the mistakes, surprises, and insights from project one — and how they shaped every build since.

ID
Ishanjot Dhahan
Jan 8, 2024 · 8 min read · Precision 3D Technologies

Every developer has a first project. The one that taught them more than any textbook. The one where the lessons came fast and hard, and where the gap between plan and reality was sometimes humbling.

Our first 3D configurator build was for a pergola company in Ontario. They had a clear problem — leads going cold between inquiry and quote — and a clear goal: give customers a way to design and price their own pergola before talking to a rep.

Simple enough, right? Here's what actually happened.

How It Started

The client came to us with two things: a stack of product photos and a spreadsheet with pricing. No 3D models, no option documentation, no integration specs. Just the raw ingredients of a product business.

We were confident. We'd done web development, interactive tools, and 3D rendering separately. This was just combining them, right?

It was not just combining them.

Mistake #1: Starting With the 3D

Our first instinct was to start with the most visually impressive thing: the 3D model. We spent the first week building a beautiful, detailed pergola model — wood grain texture, realistic shadows, real-time rotation.

The problem: we built it at fixed dimensions. When the client told us customers could order any size from 8×8 to 20×24, we had to rebuild the model from scratch with parametric geometry. One week, gone.

The lesson: Understand the full product specification before touching the 3D. The geometry needs to accommodate every possible customer configuration. Build the skeleton before the skin.

Mistake #2: Not Defining Pricing Logic First

Three weeks into the build, we asked the client for the pricing formula. What we got back was: "It depends. Width times depth times material cost, but then the roof style has a separate formula, and the attachment type changes the base price, and then the add-ons are flat rates except for the lighting package which is per linear foot..."

The pricing logic had never been written down in one place. It lived in the head of the owner and in a spreadsheet that hadn't been updated in 8 months.

We spent two full weeks just documenting and validating the pricing logic before we could code it. Two weeks that should have been in the brief.

The lesson: Pricing logic documentation is now the first deliverable in every project. We won't write a line of code until we have a complete, validated pricing matrix signed off by the client.

Lost on our first project because pricing logic wasn't documented before development started. It's now the first thing we do on every engagement.

Mistake #3: Underestimating Mobile

We built the configurator on desktop. It looked great. The 3D model was crisp, the option panels were intuitive, the pricing display was clear.

Then we tested it on an iPhone. The 3D model was tiny. The option panel took up most of the screen. Rotation controls fought with scroll. The price display was cut off.

We had to rebuild the entire layout for mobile — essentially a second product design — in the last two weeks of the project. The timeline nearly doubled.

The lesson: Design mobile first. The 3D model scales down. The interaction patterns don't. We now design every configurator starting with the mobile experience and expanding to desktop, not the other way around.

The Good Surprises

Not everything went wrong. A few things went better than expected:

The real-time pricing update — where the total changed immediately as customers made selections — produced an almost addictive engagement pattern. We watched user recordings of people sliding the width dimension back and forth, watching the price change, apparently just enjoying the interactivity. That engagement translated directly into time-on-site and conversion rate.

The lead quality from the configurator was dramatically higher than the client expected. Their sales team, which had been skeptical about the whole project, came around fast when they started getting calls from prospects who already knew exactly what they wanted and what it would cost.

How It Shaped Everything Since

Every process we use today — the brief template, the pricing matrix first rule, the mobile-first design approach, the staging and testing protocol — came directly from something that went wrong on that first project.

We also learned what makes a configurator actually work from a business perspective. The 3D quality matters. The option range matters. But the thing that determines whether it converts is whether it's aligned with how the sales team actually closes deals.

What We'd Tell Ourselves

If we could go back to the start of that first project with everything we know now, we'd say: slow down on the 3D, spend twice as long on the brief, and test on mobile before you build anything.

The technical craft matters. But the business craft — understanding the sales process, the buyer psychology, the pricing logic — is what turns a technically impressive configurator into one that actually generates revenue.

Twelve builds later, that's still the core of how we work.
